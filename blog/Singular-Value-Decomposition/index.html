Singular Value Decomposition

Convention. OO denotes a zero matrix of appropriate size.

Theorem (Singular Value Decomposition).

  1. Every matrix A∈Cm×nA∈Cm×n has a SVD:

    A=UΣV∗
    A=UΣV∗

    where U∈Cm×m,V∈Cn×nU∈Cm×m,V∈Cn×n are unitary and Σ∈Rm×nΣ∈Rm×n is "diagonal".

    Furthermore, The singular values σjσj's

    σ1≥σ2≥⋯≥σmin{m,n}≥0
    σ1≥σ2≥⋯≥σmin{m,n}≥0

    are uniquely determined.

  2. If AA is square and σjσj's are distinct, the left and right singular vectors {uj}{uj} and {vj}{vj} are unique up to a multiplicative constant with modulus 1.

Remark. The technique in the proof below remains valid when all CC is replaced by RR.

Proof of Part (1). The case that m=1m=1 or n=1n=1 is simple, let's assume m,n≥2m,n≥2. Let σ1=‖A‖2σ1=∥A∥2, then due to compactness of {x∈Cn:‖x‖2=1}{x∈Cn:∥x∥2=1} in CnCn and the continuity of the map x↦‖Ax‖2x↦∥Ax∥2, there must be v1∈Cn with ‖v1‖2=1 s.t. ‖Av1‖2=σ1, so there is u1∈Cm, ‖u1‖2=1, Av1=σ1u1. Hence ‖A‖2 is our first singular value.

Extend u1 to an o.n. basis {u1,…,um} of Cm and v1 to an o.n. basis {v1,…,vn} of Cn. Let U1 be the matrix with columns ui and V1 be that with columns vi, then (see remark next page for the explanation of B)

Corollary. Let A∈Cm×n, v∈Cn with ‖v‖2=1. Then if ‖Av‖2=‖A‖2,

w⊥v⟹Aw⊥Av.

To finish the proof let's induct on k≥4, where m+n=k. Suppose any m×n matrix with m+n=4,5,…,k−1 has SVD with uniquely determined singular values in descending order. Then for m+n=k, by induction hypothesis and according to equation (1), B=U2ΣV∗2 with unique Σ, and the existence of SVD follows from the formula:

Although Σ is unique, it is dependent on B, while B is dependent on the choice of basis. Fortunately under any changes of (u2,…,um) and (v2,…,vn) to other o.n. bases, U2 and V2 will be replaced by other unitary matrices and Σ remains unchanged, hence singular values of A are unique. The proof is almost completed by induction, except for the base case m+n=4, which is obvious by (1).

Proof of Part (2). Let's assume A∈Cn×n is square. It is clear that σ1=‖A‖2 since ‖A‖2 is the largest possible singular value of A. We first prove that if the right singular vector of σ1 is not ``unique", then σ1 is not simple, i.e., σ1 is repeated in Σ.

Let Av1=σ1u1, ‖v1‖2=‖u1‖2=1. Suppose there are other vectors w,w′∈Cn, with ‖w‖2=‖w′‖2=1 s.t. Aw=σ1w′. It follows that u1 is unique up to a complex sign. Finally since A|(Cv1)⊥:(Cv1)⊥→(Cu1)⊥, by choosing the bases of these two spaces, the uniqueness follows from induction on dimension of the square matrix.
